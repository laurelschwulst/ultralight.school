In “TWA: Total Work of Art,” we invite artists, designers, writers, programmers, and anyone else interested to shapeshift their concept across three modalities: 1) digital, 2) physical, and 3) temporal. Inspired by wide-ranging examples of design as world-building — think Bauhaus, Beyoncé, Bernadette Corporation — this workshop responds to how culture is experienced today, inviting participants to develop a core concept across multiple forms and points of access.

This 7 week workshop is led by three teachers: Stephen Kwok, Laurel Schwulst, and Bryant Wells. Meeting twice a week (on Thursdays and Sundays), each session includes conversational lectures, technical demonstrations, presentation and critique time, and guest visits. The workshop culminates in a public event in which the participants present the totality of their work.

Background

Our idea for this course began with three disparate references:

1 ) Shapeshifters was a class Stephen took at the School of the Art Institute of Chicago. Taught by Frederic Moffet, it invited students to create works for three predetermined moments of presentation: a screening, an exhibition, and an evening of performance. Within this bare but demanding structure, students were given the opportunity to realize a dynamic and multidisciplinary bodies of work alongside each other.

2 ) Touchpoints is a marketing concept which refers to the various modalities through which consumers interact with a brand. Examples of touchpoints include advertisements, social media posts, in-store experiences, and encounters with customer service representatives. Marketing professionals commonly believe that such touchpoints shape consumers’ brand perception and purchasing decisions throughout the conventionally defined decision-making process: before, during, and after purchase.

3 ) In his book A Timeless Way of Building, the architect and design theorist Christopher Alexander shares: “The finer I slice the strawberries, the more surfaces there are; the more surfaces there are, the more it tastes.” Through increasing surface area, generously considering entries, exits, and traversal patterns, an idea becomes more accessible — open to air.

All three frameworks encourage a medium and discipline agnostic approach to making.

What if we adopted these approaches for our own projects? What if we start with the interplay between modalities, rather than needing a single form to contain the essence of a work? What if there is no “primary” way to experience something anymore?

TWA: Total Work of Art

TWA: Total Work of Art “Total Work of Art” entered the chat.

According to Wikipedia, a Gesamtkunstwerk can mean a “total work of art,” a “universal artwork,” or a “synthesis of the arts.”

Richard Wagner, a composer known primarily for his operas, popularized the term throughout his career which explored combining musical, visual, and dramatic art into an all-embracing theatrical whole.

As described in Fred Turner's book The Democratic Surround, artist and Bauhaus art school educator László Moholy-Nagy argued that design schools offer the kind of training that would strengthen individual American character and American society as a whole in the face of fascism in a 1940 article titled “Relating the Parts to the Whole” —

“WE NEED IN LARGE NUMBERS a new type of person — one who sees the periphery as well as the immediate, and who can integrate his special job with the great whole of which it is a small part. This ability is a matter of everyday efficiency. It will also contribute to building a better culture.”

Suddenly, examples appeared everywhere, from Bauhaus and Corbusier to Beyoncé’s Lemonade and Kpop, Catholic Mass, it seemed as if there were no piece of culture in 2025 that didn’t have simultaneous physical, digital, and temporally-based modes of encounter. As our understanding of what could be considered gesamtkunstwerk expanded, so did our curiosity about what reconsidering this art historical concept in today’s climate might provoke. As our understanding of what could be considered Gesamtkunstwerk expanded, so did our curiosity about (the political / creative provocations of) what reconsidering this art historical concept might do today. How can we make a new form of gesamtkunstwerk today in 2025? We call it “TWA.”

Stephen Kwok makes experimental events that incorporate sculpture, live performance, digital media, and text into participatory systems. He lives in Brooklyn, teaches design at Brooklyn College, and serves as the Curator of Public Engagement at Dia Art Foundation.

Laurel Schwulst is an artist, designer, writer, educator, and technologist interested in ambient forms of design and literature, public works, and the poetic potential of the world wide web. Laurel currently teaches design at Princeton University and founded Fruitful School.

Bryant Wells is a New York-based designer who works independently and collaboratively with artists, writers, musicians, and institutions to create websites, publications, identities, and objects. His practice explores the political and cultural effects shaping networked communication, and the metaphorical and material implications of "what’s in the air.” Bryant teaches design at the Parsons School of Design and the Yale Graphic Design MFA.
