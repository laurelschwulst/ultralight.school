How does one “see” the Internet? Most of us experience it through screens, browsers, and apps — layers of interface that make it feel abstract, even invisible. Yet beneath our feet and above our heads exist the often-overlooked systems that allow it to function. Through walking plus some supplemental readings and dialogue, we will explore how the internet is not some ethereal, untouchable thing, but physically rooted — connected by underwater submarine cables and served from data centers containing many literal computers worldwide.

Walking the Internet is a 3 week class, meeting three consecutive Saturday afternooons in July in New York City to explore physical and cultural infrastructure. Taught by Alex Wolfe and Laurel Schwulst, we'll meet in Manhattan for a guided walk through a different neighborhood: the Financial District, Midtown, and Times Square/Bryant Park. Along the way, we’ll visit Privately Owned Public Spaces (POPS) and consider how the often hidden physical systems around us, along with the rhythms of daily life, and how they mirror the way we navigate and experience the digital world.

Background

TK

Alex Wolfe is an artist, writer, and educator. His writing and photography explore the relationship between movement, the built environment, and memory.

Laurel Schwulst is interested in ambient forms of design and literature, public works, and the poetic potential of the world wide web. Laurel has held design and creative direction roles at companies including Linked by Air, Kickstarter, and Are.na. She has taught design at Princeton, Yale, and her own learning initiatives: Fruitful School and Ultralight School.

(   Who might be a good fit for this class?   )

We imagine this class is ideal for anyone — designers, writers, artists, programmers, urbanists, walkers, and others — who are interested in the intersection of the physical and digital world. We encourage students who have a strong interest in the hidden systems that allow the world to function and open to hybrid class structures. This is a highly social class and best suited for people who enjoy conversation. Since this class has a strong walking component, we imagine it’s best for people who are comfortable walking distances up to 5 miles in potentially hot and humid weather typical during NYC summers.
